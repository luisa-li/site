math3545: graph theory

hexagons are the bestagons!

proving that a plane can only be tiled with three types of regular polygons (triangles, squares and hexagons) through euler's formula, the handshake lemma and the faceshake lemma, all of which are elementary results of studying planar graphs.

euler's formula states that for a planar graph:

\[ e + 2 = n + f\]

where \(G = (V, E)\), \( e = |E| \), \( n = |V| \), and \(f\) is the number of faces the planar graph \(G\).

the handshake lemma states that the sum of node degrees in a graph is equal to double the number of edges, which is an intuitive result:

\[ \sum_{v \in V} deg(v) = 2e \]

and lastly, the faceshake lemma (as dubbed by my professor) is the equivalent of the handshake lemma for the faces in a planar graph, stating that the sum of face degrees (or the number of edges that make up a face) is equivalent also to double the number of edges. also quite intuitive:

\[ \sum_{f \in F} deg(f) = 2e \]

the handshake and faceshake lemma are simplified to \( nk = 2e \) and \( fl = 2e \), respectively, with the original assumptions that we are tiling with regular polygons. \( k \) is the degree of each node, and \(l\) is the number of sides of the polygon that we wish to tile with.

it turns out that that is enough for us to concretely say something about the polygons we can use to tile a 2D plane with. with some algebraic manipulations and number theory, we find eventually that the only integer \(k\) and \(l\) that satisfy constraints are \(k = 6, l = 3\), \(k = 4, l = 4\), \(k = 3, l = 6\), which corresponds to triangles, squares and hexagons, respectively.

isn't it pretty cool that elementary results in graph theory can tell you something so general about regular tilings? :D

cs3800: theory of computation

proving that the traveling salesperson problem (TSP) is NP complete.

back in highschool i did a long project on various exploration and exploitation strategies in genetic algorithms evaluated on the traveling salesperson problem, and i distinctly remember writing in my final paper that TSP is NP-complete, without really understanding what it meant. it genuinely felt like a full circle moment when learned about complexity classes in our final lectures. the actual proof, which involved a polynomial time mapping reduction from the hamiltonian cycle problem, was not exactly exciting, but finally acquiring enough background to fully understand something previously out of reach was satisfying.

i also saw a bunch of very interesting, creative and sometimes unintuitive proofs in this class. notably, the proof that language \(L\) is regular iff \(\exists\) a regular expression \(R\) s.t \(L(R) = L\), and the reduction from 3SAT to the subset sum problem.
